<h2 style="line-height:1.2;">Did you know that a poorly structured spreadsheet can be unreadable for someone using a screen reader &mdash; even if all the data is present?</h2>

Spreadsheets are often used to share critical data, reports, and analysis. Without proper structure, users who rely on assistive technology, or who need to clearly understand the structure, may not be able to interpret or navigate the information.

Accessible spreadsheets ensure that all users can access, understand, and use the data effectively.

## Accessible Spreadsheet Design Checklist
<ol>
  <li>
    <h3>Structure and Table Design</h3>
    <ol>
      <li>Define data as a formal table (when supported by your authoring tool) so assistive technologies can recognize structure.</li>
      <li>Use clear visible and programmatic row and column headers to establish relationships between data &mdash; making the text bold is not sufficient.</li>
      <li>Keep each table as a single, continuous range without breaks, blank rows, or unrelated data.</li>
      <li>Use a simple, clean table structure by avoiding merged cells and unnecessary blank rows or columns, which can disrupt reading order and navigation.</li>
      <li>If multiple tables appear on a single sheet, clearly separate and label each one.</li>
    </ol>
  </li>
  <li>
    <h3>Naming and Labeling</h3>
    <ol>
      <li>Provide a meaningful table name that clearly describes its purpose.</li>
      <li>Use descriptive sheet (tab) names instead of generic labels such as "Sheet1".</li>
      <li>
        Include a title or summary at the top of the spreadsheet that explains:
        <ol>
          <li>The purpose of the data</li>
          <li>How the data should be interpreted</li>
        </ol>
      </li>
    </ol>
  </li>
  <li>
    <h3>Layout and Organization</h3>
    <ol>
      <li>Organize content logically so it follows a clear and predictable reading order.</li>
      <li>Use formatting (such as borders, spacing, and alignment) instead of blank rows or columns to create visual separation.</li>
      <li>Ensure content flows consistently from top to bottom and left to right.</li>
    </ol>
  </li>
  <li>
    <h3>Visual Design and Color</h3>
    <ol>
      <li>Ensure sufficient color contrast between foreground text and background.</li>
      <li>Do not rely on color alone to convey meaning.</li>
      <li>
        Use additional indicators in charts such as:
        <ol>
          <li>Text labels</li>
          <li>Symbols or icons</li>
          <li>Patterns or styling cues</li>
        </ol>
      </li>
    </ol>
  </li>
  <li>
    <h3>Assistive Technology Compatibility</h3>
    <ol>
      <li>Test the spreadsheet with assistive technologies or accessibility checking tools to verify that tables, headers, and data relationships are correctly conveyed.</li>
      <li>Verify that data relationships (such as headers and grouped content) are programmatically associated and correctly read.</li>
    </ol>
  </li>
  <li>
    <h3>Tool Usage (When Available)</h3>
    <ol>
      <li>Use your spreadsheet tool's "Format as Table" (or equivalent) feature when supported to enhance structure and accessibility.</li>
      <li>Confirm that the tool preserves headers, naming, and relationships when exporting or sharing the file.</li>
    </ol>
  </li>
</ol>

### Quick check

As the author of the spreadsheet, it is your responsibility to ensure that all users can access the information. Before sharing your workbook with others, ask yourself: *Can someone understand and navigate this spreadsheet using only a keyboard and a screen reader?*

If not, simplify the structure and clarify the data.
