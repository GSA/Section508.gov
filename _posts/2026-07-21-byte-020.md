<h2 style="line-height:1.2;">Did you know that millions of Americans rely entirely on captions and transcripts to understand video and audio content?</h2>

<a href="https://www.nidcd.nih.gov/health/statistics/quick-statistics-hearing?utm_source=chatgpt.com" target="_blank" class="usa-link--external">Approximately 15% of American adults (37.5 million) ages 18 and over report some trouble hearing</a>, often making captions and other text equivalents the first and most impactful step toward making media usable for everyone --- including people who are deaf or hard of hearing. But they also benefit a much broader audience: people in noisy environments, multilingual users, and anyone who prefers reading over listening.

## 5 Quick Tips for Better Captions

Content creators should avoid many common issues by following a few core practices:
<ol class="usa-process-list itad-process-list--wide">
  <li class="usa-process-list__item">
    <h3 class="usa-process-list__heading">Sync captions with the audio</h3>
    <p>Captions should appear at the same time as the speech or sound they represent with no lag or guessing.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="usa-process-list__item">
    <h3 class="usa-process-list__heading">Capture <em>all</em> meaningful audio</h3>
    <p>Include:</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Verbatim dialogue; or equivalent where appropriate</li>
      <li>Sound effects (e.g., <em>[door opens]</em>)</li>
      <li>Music cues when relevant</li>
    </ul>
    <p>Captions aren’t just subtitles as they convey the full experience.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="usa-process-list__item">
    <h3 class="usa-process-list__heading">Keep captions readable</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Limit to 2 lines at a time</li>
      <li>Aim for ~45 characters per line</li>
      <li>Keep them on screen long enough to read</li>
    </ul>
  </li>

  <li class="usa-process-list__item">
    <h3 class="usa-process-list__heading">Use consistent formatting</h3>
    <p>Be consistent when identifying:</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Speakers</li>
      <li>Sound effects</li>
      <li>Tone (e.g., <em>[whispering]</em>)</li>
    </ul>
    <p>Consistency helps users follow along more easily.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="usa-process-list__item">
    <h3 class="usa-process-list__heading">Don’t rely on auto-captions alone</h3>
    <p>Auto-generated captions are a helpful starting point, but they often include:</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Incorrect word substitutions</li>
      <li>Missing sound effects</li>
      <li>Missing speaker changes</li>
      <li>Missing punctuation</li>
      <li>Spelling errors</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ol>

## Plan Ahead for Better Captions and Transcripts
Accessibility starts early in the production process, and the choices made before and during recording directly shape how effective captions and transcripts will be later. Planning ahead --- such as avoiding overlapping speech, maintaining a steady speaking pace, keeping key on-screen text out of the lower third of the screen, and allowing natural pauses --- can make captions easier to generate and significantly more accurate. These simple production practices reduce downstream rework and help ensure your content is clear, usable, and accessible from the start.

To go further, captions and transcripts should be treated as essential communication tools, not afterthoughts. A strong transcript provides a complete, text-based version of your content, including meaningful spoken audio and important visual context, and should be easy to find alongside the media in an accessible format. Captions and transcripts can also be enhanced with context cues --- such as tone, language changes, or unclear audio --- to preserve meaning beyond the spoken words.
